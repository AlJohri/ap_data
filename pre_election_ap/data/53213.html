Vern Buchanan was born in Detroit, and currently lives in Longboat Key, Fla. He earned a bachelor's in business administration from Cleary University in Ann Arbor, Mich., and a master's degree from the University of Detroit.

He served six years in the Air National Guard.

Buchanan is an auto dealer and self-made millionaire. He has served as chairman of the Sarasota Chamber of Commerce and chairman of the Florida Chamber of Commerce. He also served on the U.S. Chamber of Commerce's Board of Directors.

He was elected to the U.S. House in 2006.

Buchanan and his wife, Sandy, have two children.

Profile

Vern Buchanan is the only member of the Florida delegation with a seat on the powerful Ways and Means Committee in the 112th Congress. He holds a key leadership role as vice chairman of that committee.

He introduced a bill in March 2011 designed to put illicit pain clinics out of business in Florida, the nation's leading illegal supplier of addictive pain pills such as oxycodone. Buchanan's bill would increase maximum prison sentences for pill mill operators from 10 years to 20 years and triple the potential fine from $1 million to $3 million.

A multimillionaire auto dealer, Buchanan has struggled with a series of ethical issues over recent years.

The House Ethics Committee reported in July 2012 that Buchanan omitted information on his financial disclosure forms over four years, but the panel took no action because the errors were corrected.

The disclosure case wasn't the only issue Buchanan had before the Ethics Committee, which reported in May 2012 that there was substantial reason to believe the Sarasota Republican attempted to get a former business partner to lie to the Federal Election Commission in violation of federal law and a House rule.

Buchanan voted against the 2010 health care reform bill and said the June 2012 Supreme Court decision upholding the constitutionality of the legislation does not change the fact that it should be repealed.

"While the Supreme Court did not declare the President's government takeover of health care unconstitutional, the law remains unworkable and unaffordable," he said. "Congress must work in a bipartisan manner to repeal and replace the health care law with common-sense reforms that lower the cost of health insurance and ensure that the American people can go to the doctor of their choice."

Buchanan also voted against the 2009 approximately $800 billion economic stimulus package, saying the bill doesn't go far enough to create jobs and will increase the national debt.

He introduced the Buchanan Balanced Budget Amendment Act in 2007 and the Earmark Accountability and Reform Act in 2009. Buchanan also introduced legislation to stop automatic pay raises for members of Congress until the budget is balanced.

Key issues for Buchanan are strengthening intelligence and domestic counterterrorism and securing the nation's southern border. He is opposed to a guest worker program that would lead to citizenship.

Buchanan opposes abortion, embryonic stem cell research and gay marriage. "I will work to promote traditional family values and I will be a strong defender of life," Buchanan said.

Buchanan is a member of the House Small Business Committee and serves as the Republican leader of the Subcommittee on Finance and Tax. He also serves on the House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and is a member of the Subcommittee on Surface Transportation. In addition, Buchanan serves on the House Veterans' Affairs Committee and won the Veterans of Foreign Wars Legislative Achievement Award.

He spent $2 million of his reported $50 million fortune to win the 2006 Republican primary.

Buchanan succeeded Republican Rep. Katherine Harris in the 13th District. Harris ran for U.S. Senate and lost.

During his 2006 election campaign, Democrats made an issue of a lawsuit Buchanan filed against the developers of Sarasota's Ritz-Carlton Hotel. A judge sealed the file and removed the lawsuit from the public record at Buchanan's request. But he has denied any wrongdoing and asked the judge to unseal the record after the primary.
