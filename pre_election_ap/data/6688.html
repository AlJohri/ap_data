Tony Cardenas was born and raised in Los Angeles' San Fernando Valley, the youngest of 11 children. His father, a Mexican immigrant, worked as a farm laborer before starting his own gardening business. He received a bachelor's degree in engineering from the University of California-Santa Barbara.

Cardenas worked in real estate before deciding to run for the state Assembly in 1996. He has said he was inspired to seek office after learning that no one had ever been elected to the state Legislature from the modest, largely Hispanic neighborhood of Pacoima, where he grew up.

After term limits forced him to leave the Assembly, Cardenas was elected to represent his San Fernando Valley neighborhood on the Los Angeles City Council in 2003. He was re-elected to the City Council in 2007 and again in 2011.

Cardenas, whose brother-in-law is prominent Chicano author Luis J. Rodriguez, lives in the San Fernando Valley, in a neighborhood not far from where he grew up, with his wife, Norma, and their children.

Profile

Tony Cardenas, a Los Angeles city councilman, is seeking election in 2012 to California's newly redrawn 29th Congressional District, which now takes in much of Los Angeles' northeast San Fernando Valley, including the Panorama City area where Cardenas lives. The district's current congressman, Rep. Adam Schiff is seeking election to the newly redrawn 28th district. Cardenas faces conservative independent candidate David Hernandez in the November general election.

Cardenas, who is serving his third term on the City Council, is a lifetime resident of the area and has said creating jobs and making the streets safe are the most important issues facing the sprawling section of modest, aging single-family homes, apartment buildings and small businesses that blanket the blue-collar, heavily Hispanic section of Los Angeles.

Before he was elected to the City Council in 2003, Cardenas served six years in the state Assembly and was chairman of the Assembly's Budget Committee. He says that experience, coupled with his years running a real estate business before going into politics, have given him the ability to cut through red tape and institute reforms needed to make Los Angeles more business-friendly.

He takes credit for recently bringing a new food manufacturing plant to the district's Panorama City neighborhood that created 400 new jobs.

As chairman of the City Council's ad hoc committee on business tax reform, Cardenas worked to simplify Los Angeles' business tax categories, reducing them from 75 to seven.

During his time in the state Legislature he also worked to secure more than $650 million for new school construction in Los Angeles.

As vice chairman of the City Council's Public Safety Committee he worked to create gang-intervention programs for local neighborhoods that involved members of the community. He likes to note that although he and his 10 brothers and sisters were raised in one of the city's most gang-ridden neighborhoods, none of them joined street gangs.

During his first two years on the City Council, Cardenas also orchestrated the cleanup of more than 5,000 tons of trash from San Fernando Valley streets, and he continues to periodically lead neighborhood cleanup efforts.
