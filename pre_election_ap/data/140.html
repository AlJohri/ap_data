Loretta Sanchez was born in Lynwood, Calif., and now lives in Garden Grove. She earned a bachelor's from Chapman University in 1982 and a master's in business administration from American University in 1984.

She was a financial manager at the Orange County Transportation Authority; an assistant vice president at Fieldman, Rollap and Associates; and an associate at Booz, Allen, and Hamilton.

She won election to the U.S. House in 1996. Her sister, Linda Sanchez, won a seat in Congress in 2002. The two are the first sister act in Congress.

They suffered a family tragedy in 2008, when their brother, Henry Sanchez, and his girlfriend, Penny Avila, died in a boating accident in the waters off Los Angeles.

Sanchez married Army Col. Jack Einwechter in a private ceremony in Orange County in 2011.

Profile

Loretta Sanchez is a member of the Blue Dog Coalition, a Democratic group that supports conservative fiscal policies in Washington. She has worked to repeal the estate tax and increase the amount of federal loans available for small businesses.

Sanchez, the daughter of Mexican immigrants, praised the Supreme Court in 2012 for striking down large portions of Arizona's controversial immigration law, calling it "a victory and vindication that state and local municipalities simply do not have the authority to set the law on immigration matters."

She also sponsored in 2012 a bill in the House dubbed the Startup Act that would let some foreign graduate students remain in the United States after finishing their studies so they can adjust their status to legal residents. But undocumented graduates who otherwise meet the educational criteria can't apply.

She voted in 2009 for the approximately $800 billion economic stimulus package.

Sanchez introduced legislation that would allow for all ships coming into U.S. ports to be tracked to allow for greater security.

Her district includes one of the nation's largest Vietnamese communities, and she has become a vocal opponent of Vietnam's human rights record.

She held a congressional hearing with California Rep. Zoe Lofgren on media censorship in Vietnam. She founded the Congressional Caucus on Vietnam, and introduced a resolution calling for religious freedom following Vietnam's crackdown on the banned Unified Buddhist Church.

She rejected an opportunity in 2006 to join a congressional trade delegation to Vietnam because she would not have been permitted to visit some regions or meet with political and religious dissidents. The following year, she was among several House members who wrote a letter to then-Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice asking for her help in gaining the release of two political activists arrested in Vietnam.

She said in September 2008 that human rights have continued to deteriorate in Vietnam, pointing to the detention of students and bloggers who expressed concerns about government policies.

Sanchez made headlines in early 2007 when she quit the Congressional Hispanic Caucus, saying its chairman had called her a "whore."

Sanchez and her sister wrote a joint memoir in 2008 called "Dream in Color: How the Sanchez Sisters Are Making History in Congress." The book traces their paths to Washington, where they found themselves a minority within a minority — Hispanic women in an institution dominated by white men.

Sanchez successfully lobbied to add to the 2009 National Defense Authorization Act a sexual assault database to raise public awareness of sexual abuse in the military and encourage accountability within the armed services.

Sanchez included a provision in the 2010 National Defense Authorization Act to address concerns that support units with female service members do not receive adequate combat-skills training, thereby putting the entire unit in danger.

Her 2006 re-election victory came on the heels of a campaign marked by accusations of voter intimidation. Her opponent's campaign sent letters to Hispanic immigrants claiming they could be deported or jailed for voting. Evidence was seized but no charges were filed, as investigators concluded there was no criminal intent.
