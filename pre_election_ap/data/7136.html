John Garamendi was born in Camp Blanding, Fla., and raised in Mokelumne Hill, Calif. He currently resides in Walnut Grove. He earned a bachelor's degree in business from the University of California-Berkeley and a master's degree in business administration from Harvard University.

Garamendi was a second team All-American offensive guard in football, a two-time member of the All-Pacific Coast Conference team and the 1964 West Coast heavyweight wrestling champion.

He was a Peace Corps volunteer in Ethiopia, and he served in both the state House and the state Senate. He was elected lieutenant governor in 2006.

He joined the U.S. House in 2009, winning a special election to fill the seat of Rep. Ellen Tauscher who left for a position in President Barack Obama's administration.

He and his wife, Patti, have six children.

Profile

John Garamendi — a handsome, wealthy and idealistic politician — has drawn comparisons to his political idol, former President John F. Kennedy.

The representative for California's 10th District, Garamendi joined the U.S. House in 2009, winning a special election to fill the seat of Rep. Ellen Tauscher, who left for a position in President Barack Obama's administration.

In 2012, following dramatic redistricting in California, he opted to run for a seat in the new 3rd District slightly to the north of the district he currently represents.

Garamendi campaigned in 2009 on a broad agenda that included everything from climate change to investment in higher education, overcoming his opponent's effort to capitalize on voter angst over bailouts for automakers and banks.

In June 2012 Garamendi co-wrote legislation to prevent the president from branding a U.S. citizen as an enemy of the state and indefinitely detaining them. The legislation would ensure that all individuals accused of being associated with terrorist organizations will be transferred immediately to federal courts for charges and trial.

"Anyone can be falsely accused of a crime. Everyone accused of a crime deserves a fair trial," Garamendi said.

He has followed through on his campaign promise to be a solid vote in favor of Obama's domestic agenda. He backed the 2010 health care reform bill, the 2010 repeal of "don't ask, don't tell," the 2010 overhaul of financial industry regulations and the approximately $800 billion economic stimulus package.

Garamendi wrote unsuccessful legislation in April 2010 in the wake of BP's Deepwater Horizon oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico that called for a permanent ban on new offshore drilling in federal waters near California, Oregon and Washington.

Garamendi served for several years in the California Legislature, where he was known as a top budget writer. During his time as a state lawmaker, he ran unsuccessfully for statewide office, losing two gubernatorial bids.

He was tapped by President Bill Clinton to become deputy secretary of the interior in 1995.

He served twice as state insurance commissioner, and one of his biggest political liabilities remains his handling of a failed insurance company.

Garamendi approved as commissioner the sale of Executive Life and its junk bond portfolio for $3.25 billion to an investor group financed by French bank Credit Lyonnais. The bonds were later resold for billions more. The state Department of Insurance sued the French investors and recovered only a portion of what Garamendi said was due.

During his time as lieutenant governor, Garamendi served as chairman of the three-member State Lands Commission, where he opposed raising tax revenue through expanded offshore oil drilling despite the state's ongoing budget deficits. He led the commission in June 2009 in passing a resolution urging the Legislature to oppose oil drilling off the coast of Santa Barbara, which supporters said could bring in $1.8 billion to state coffers over time.

Throughout more than 30 years in public office, one thing has eluded Garamendi: the governor's seat.

He announced plans to run as a replacement for then-Gov. Gray Davis during the 2003 recall election but withdrew under pressure from fellow Democrats.

He was contemplating a fourth run for the governorship when Tauscher gave up her House seat and he won the election to replace her.
