Rich Nugent was born in Evergreen Park, Ill., and now lives in Spring Hill, Fla. Nugent joined the Illinois Air National Guard in 1969 and served six years. He received a bachelor's degree from Saint Leo University, a master's degree in public administration from Troy University and graduated from the FBI National Academy in 1991.

Nugent began his law enforcement career in 1972 in Illinois. He joined the Hernando County Sheriff's Office after moving to Florida in 1984. Nugent gained command experience in all areas of the department and served as operations bureau commander. He was elected sheriff in 2000.

He was elected to Congress in 2010.

Nugent and his wife, Wendy, have three sons.

Profile

Rich Nugent was handpicked by retiring Republican Rep. Ginny Brown-Waite to succeed her in Florida's 5th Congressional District in 2010. In 2012, as a result of redistricting following the 2010 census, Nugent is now in the 11th Congressional District.

Upon taking office, Nugent tried to opt out of the congressional pension program but was unable to do so because of federal law. He immediately filed his first and as of August 2012, only bill, titled the "Congress is Not a Career Act," which would let members decline to participate in the pension program. The House has taken no action on the bill.

Nugent is among a group of conservative Republicans who rode into office on the tea party tide of 2010. He was outspoken in his support of a 2011 bill that would have slashed spending by trillions of dollars and required a balanced budget constitutional amendment in exchange for an increase in the nation's borrowing limit. "Washington has a spending addiction," Nugent said in floor debate. The House passed the measure, but the Senate turned it down.

Nugent also is in the forefront of an effort to hold Attorney General Eric Holder in contempt of the House for refusing to disclose internal Justice Department documents related to a botched gun-running investigation known as Operation Fast and Furious. President Barack Obama's administration is claiming executive privilege. Nugent tied the issue to the death of a Border Patrol agent because two guns involved in the gunrunning operation were found near the scene of his death. "A man died serving his country and we have a right to know what the federal government's hand was in that," Nugent said.

The former sheriff also was in the middle of a dispute over manatee protection back home. At the urging of local business people and politicians he threatened to bar the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service from spending any money to implement speed limits on boats in Kings Bay in Citrus County to protect the endangered species. The agency relented and agreed to allow boaters to go up to 25 miles per hour in the daytime during a 10-week summer period in part of the bay.

A self-described fiscal conservative, Nugent chose to pay for his own health insurance rather that receive government-paid coverage and is one of several lawmakers who sleeps in his office. The latter was a "financial decision" to offset the cost of paying for his own insurance, Nugent said.

Nugent has signed on as a co-sponsor to a raft of conservative legislation including a repeal of the 2010 federal health care reform bill, a ban on taxpayer funding of abortions and something called the Better Use of Light Bulbs Act, which would repeal energy efficiency requirements for lighting.
