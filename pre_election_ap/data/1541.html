Carolyn Maloney was born in Greensboro, N.C., and now lives in New York City. She earned a bachelor's degree in history and literature from Greensboro College.

Maloney was a public school teacher in New York City from 1972 to 1974. She became a special assistant in career and occupational education with the city's Board of Education in 1974. She left that post in 1978 to become a legislative aide to the state Assembly Housing Committee.

In 1979, Maloney went to work for the state Senate minority leader, first as director of his advisory council and later as director of special projects. She served on the New York City Council from 1982 to 1992.

She was elected to the U.S. House in 1992.

Maloney was widowed in 2009 after her husband, Clifton, died in a hiking accident in Tibet. She has two daughters.

Profile

Carolyn Maloney has represented Manhattan's east side and part of the borough of Queens since winning New York's 14th District U.S. House seat in 1992. Due to redistricting following the 2010 census, she will be running in the 12th District in 2012.

The Sept. 11 terror attacks in lower Manhattan have helped shape much of Maloney's agenda. At the end of 2010, she helped push through the James Zadroga 9/11 Health and Compensation Act, named after a police officer who died of a respiratory disease he contracted during the 9/11 rescue operations. The bill, which provided $4.2 billion to cover illnesses related to the attacks, was opposed by Republicans who worried about its financing.

She has become a sharp critic of Republicans who, in 2012, held Attorney General Eric Holder in contempt of Congress for refusing to release documents on the federal gun operation dubbed "Operation Fast and Furious." In June, she accused the GOP-led House of being on a witch hunt that would not end until after the November 2012 elections.

At the end of 2011, she pushed an amendment to the National Defense Authorization Act of 2012, allowing Freedom of Information Act inquiries and disclosures of non-security related information about defense operations. The push came after a water contamination scare at North Carolina's Camp Lejeune.

As a member of the House Financial Services Committee and immediate past chairwoman of the Joint Economic Committee, Maloney helped push the financial regulation reform bill backed by President Barack Obama and passed by Congress in July 2010. She also introduced a bill, signed by Obama in spring 2010, to provide new consumer protections for credit card holders.

Maloney had to calculate the political risk of such a stance: Many of her constituents work on Wall Street and have been wary of proposed federal reforms. But she insists Wall Street — and the state of New York — will benefit from sensible financial regulation.

Maloney has been a strong supporter of abortion rights and other women's issues throughout her congressional tenure. She has sponsored legislation guaranteeing the right to breastfeed at the workplace, on federal property and at national parks.

A reliable Democratic partisan, Maloney joined in the fray after conservative radio talk show host Rush Limbaugh in early 2012 referred to Georgetown student Sandra Fluke a prostitute and slut for suggesting employees of religion-affiliated institutions have access to health insurance that covers birth control. At a hearing with Democratic leader Nancy Pelosi, Maloney prominently displayed a photo of five religious leaders, all men and all appearing at the invitation of the Republican majority at a previous hearing where Fluke was barred from testifying. In the photo, Fluke is visible in the background, sitting in the visitors' section.

Maloney voted for the 2009 approximately $800 billion economic stimulus package and the 2010 health care reform bill that was recently upheld by the Supreme Court.
