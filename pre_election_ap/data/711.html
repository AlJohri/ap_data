Randy Forbes was born in Chesapeake, Va., where he still resides. He earned a bachelor's degree from Randolph-Macon College in 1974 and a law degree from the University of Virginia in 1977.

Forbes is a former state GOP chairman who served in both houses of the Virginia General Assembly. He served seven years in the House of Delegates and three years in the state Senate, where he served as floor leader.

He won a special election to the U.S. House in 2001.

Forbes and his wife, Shirley, have four children.

Profile

Randy Forbes was elected to Congress in 2001 with a swell of support from Republicans who saw in him a chance to claim a seat formerly held by a Democrat.

Vice President Dick Cheney stumped on Forbes' behalf, and President George W. Bush and his mother, former first lady Barbara Bush, both recorded telephone campaign messages for Forbes.

"We couldn't have done it without you," Forbes told President Bush after his victory.

Virginia's 4th District seat, which had been left vacant by the death of Democratic Rep. Norman Sisisky, represents a good swath of Democratic-leaning areas of Southside and Tidewater.

In his 2012 quest for a seventh term, he will face Ella Ward, a Chesapeake city councilwoman and former state Board of Education member in a district rendered even more conservative by redistricting.

Forbes voted against the 2010 health care reform bill and has called for a repeal of the law. He called the 2012 Supreme Court decision upholding the law disappointing and said it "underscores the importance of electing officials who support individual freedoms and a more limited vision of government."

He urged the White House in 2012 to fire Secret Service Director Mark Sullivan after reports surfaced about prostitutes with Secret Service agents, officers and supervisors in Cartagena, Colombia.

Forbes introduced in early 2009 legislation to establish an optional flat tax. He voted against the 2008 bailout of the financial services industry.

He joined two other Republican congressmen from Virginia, Tom Wolf and Eric Cantor, in introducing legislation to prohibit the transfer of prisoners to the state from Guantanamo Bay, Cuba.

Forbes heads the Congressional China Caucus and is known for casting a skeptical and critical eye on the emerging economic superpower.

"The Chinese have for years been wielding a lot of influence," Forbes told The Washington Post in January 2010. "They've liked to do it under the radar. But as there's been more light shed on it, they've had to change their ways."

Forbes has supported traditional conservative causes in Congress, including bans on flag burning and various forms of abortion and support for tax reductions.

He sponsored legislation in 2005 to toughen punishment for gang violence, and he has advocated for immigration reform and for making English the United States' official language.

The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers undertook at Forbes' request a 2007 study of the Blackwater River's tendency to flood U.S. Route 460 and the communities of Zuni and Franklin, and why the problem seemed to be getting worse.

Forbes had a prominent role in an unsuccessful Republican effort to broaden a Democratic bill dealing with voting fraud, particularly by noncitizens. He argued that illegal voting defrauded those with voting rights. Obama, then a senator, sponsored the Senate version of the bill Forbes targeted.
