Louie Gohmert was born in Pittsburgh, Texas, grew up in Mount Pleasant and now resides in Tyler. He earned a bachelor's degree in history at Texas A&M University and a law degree at Baylor University.

After graduation from law school, he briefly worked as an assistant district attorney for Titus, Morris and Camp counties while awaiting his active military duty orders.

Following his tour of duty as a captain in the Army, he built a private civil litigation practice, specializing in mediation. In 1992, he was elected judge for the 7th District Court in Tyler.

Gov. Rick Perry appointed Gohmert to fill an unexpired six-month term as chief justice of the 12th Court of Appeals in July 2002.

Gohmert was elected to the U.S. House in 2004.

He and his wife, Kathy, have three daughters.

Profile

Louie Gohmert is a co-sponsor of legislation introduced in April 2010 that would expand operations to find missing children, senior citizens or adults with disabilities.

The bill — titled A Child Is Missing Alert and Recovery Center Act — is also supported by Democratic Rep. Ron Klein and is designed to speed response time after a person is reported missing.

As the House was headed in late 2011 toward a standoff on a payroll tax increase that was set to take effect on Jan. 1, 2012, Gohmert inserted himself into the debate, saying that though a break on the tax had failed to create any jobs during the previous year, he supported extending it for another 12 months because "it's tough to raise taxes when you're in a down economy."

Gohmert voted against a 2009 House measure that indefinitely extended the estate tax.

"After someone dies and someone comes in and steals from them, we consider that in most society reprehensible," Gohmert said. "I have sentenced people personally to prison for doing that."

Gohmert defended Rep. Joe Wilson, days after Wilson shouted, "You lie!" during a 2010 speech to Congress by President Barack Obama, saying that the president had offended Congress — instead of the other way around.

"He comes in here talking about a lie. ... He says we're making wild claims," Gohmert said of Obama's allegations that Republicans were mischaracterizing Democrats' health care bills. "That's no way to act when you're invited into somebody else's house."

Being careful not to violate rules by saying the president was lying in his speech, Gohmert added, "Whoever put that line in his teleprompter should know that that's not true."

Gohmert won election to the U.S. House as a conservative Republican in a reconfigured eastern Texas district drawn to favor the GOP. He is showing no signs of losing his grip on the seat.

As a judge, Gohmert angered AIDS activists in 1996 when he ordered a man with the disease to obtain written, informed consent from all future sex partners as part of his probation in a car theft conviction.

The House passed in 2005 a Gohmert-sponsored bill increasing punishments for crimes against judges, their families and other court personnel. Gohmert began working on the measure after a man opened fire at the Smith County courthouse in Tyler with an assault rifle. The man's ex-wife and a bystander who fired back were killed.

Among Gohmert's congressional priorities are strengthening the military and securing the borders, reducing wasteful government spending, advocating gun-ownership rights, standing up for anti-abortion policies and defending heterosexual marriage.

Gohmert showed an interest in politics in his hometown of Mount Pleasant and served as president of his class at Baylor School of Law.

He is a ranking member of the Judiciary Subcommittee on Crime, Terrorism and Homeland Security.
