Brian Higgins was born in Buffalo, N.Y., where he still resides. He earned a bachelor's degree in political science and a master's degree in history from Buffalo State College. He also earned a master's in public policy and administration from Harvard University in 1996.

He worked as a laborer for his father and uncles, who worked as bricklayers, on weekend side jobs they took to help make ends meet.

He later served as a lecturer in the history and economics departments at Buffalo State College.

Higgins was elected to the Buffalo Common Council in 1987 and served for six years. In 1998, he was elected to the New York Assembly.

He was elected to the U.S. House in 2004.

Higgins and his wife, Mary Jane, have two children.

Profile

Brian Higgins loves to talk publicly about his modest upbringing and his father and uncle, who were bricklayers.

He won election to New York's 27th Congressional District in 2004 after Republican Rep. Jack Quinn announced he was retiring. The seat was considered safely Republican, but only as long as Quinn — who could sway Democratic voters, particularly blue-collar Reagan Democrats — was on the ballot. There were more than 200,000 Democrats in the district and 124,000 Republicans.

Redistricting put him in a redrawn 26th District for his 2012 re-election effort, but the enrollment advantage followed. The new district has 240,000 registered Democrats, compared to 95,000 Republicans.

Higgins, whose district includes the U.S.-Canadian border, was appointed to the Foreign Affairs and Homeland Security committees for the 112th Congress, which began in January 2011.

In a speech on the House floor in July 2012, he accused the Republican majority of "holding the American people and its economy hostage in a cynical ploy to keep the economy from growing and creating jobs."

After the U.S. Supreme Court upheld the 2010 health care reform law in June 2012, Higgins released a statement enumerating how New York residents already were benefitting from the law.

Also that month, he took to the House floor and called for the removal of Postmaster General Patrick R. Donahoe over his handling of the Postal Service's restructuring efforts, which included a proposal to close a Buffalo processing center.

Higgins' first term representing the Buffalo-area district was marked with a private victory in negotiating with New York State's Power Authority.

He trumpeted an agreement reached in December 2005 ensuring Erie County would receive $279 million over 50 years as part of the power authority's re-licensing. The deal was hailed as a major victory for the cash-strapped county, and the money has helped spur what has long been Higgins' main goal, a waterfront redevelopment project that he says is the cornerstone of an economic turnaround for Buffalo.

As part of the waterfront development project, Higgins, in July 2010, issued an ultimatum to Bass Pro executives, giving them two weeks to finalize their commitment to building a store in Buffalo, something the company had talked about for nine years. As the deadline approached, Bass Pro wrote back to Higgins pulling out of the project altogether.

He is co-chair of the Revitalizing Old Cities Task Force, which focuses on federal policy to address economic challenges of older industrial cities and towns.

He was seen as a contender for Hillary Rodham Clinton's Senate seat after she was picked to serve as President Barack Obama's secretary of state, but the seat went to then-U.S. Rep. Kirsten Gillibrand.
