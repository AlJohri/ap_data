Rob Wittman was born in Washington, D.C., and now resides in Montross, Va. He earned a bachelor's from Virginia Polytechnic Institute in 1981, a master's from the University of North Carolina in 1990 and a doctorate from Virginia Commonwealth University in 2002.

Wittman was an environmental health specialist for local health departments before becoming a field director for the Virginia Health Department's Division of Shellfish Sanitation.

He served 10 years on the Montross Town Council — four of those years as mayor — and 10 years on the Westmoreland County Board of Supervisors. He was elected to the Virginia House of Delegates in 2005.

Wittman won a special election to the U.S. House in 2007, filling the seat left vacant by the death of Republican Rep. Jo Ann Davis.

Wittman and his wife, Kathryn, have two children.

Profile

Rob Wittman joined Congress in 2007 after winning a special election to replace Republican Rep. Jo Ann Davis, who died after a two-year battle with breast cancer.

Wittman's 2007 campaign themes included cracking down on illegal immigration, supporting a strong national defense and slowing the rate of federal spending. He voted against the 2008 financial services industry bailout and against President Barack Obama's budget in early 2009. He voted in favor, however, of the 2009 economic stimulus package.

Wittman is the ranking Republican on the House Armed Services subcommittee on oversight and investigation, and he has questioned the Pentagon's efforts to defend U.S. troops in Iraq and Afghanistan from improvised explosive devices that have taken a deadly toll.

He asked Lt. Gen. Thomas Metz whether the effort Metz headed to defeat IEDs was being properly funded, noting that appropriations had declined from slightly more than $4 billion in fiscal 2008 to $2.1 billion in fiscal 2009.

Wittman joined other Virginia political leaders in July 2010 in opposing a possible recommendation to eliminate the Norfolk-based Joint Forces Command. The command employs more than 5,000 civilian and military personnel.

Wittman served on the Montross Town Council, where he developed a municipal sewer system and a computerized tax billing system. His tenure on the Westmoreland County Board of Supervisors was marked by the construction of three new libraries.

Wittman focused during his short stint in the state legislature on issues pertaining to fisheries. He has continued his focus on watermen's issues while in Congress, seeking state and federal aid for those hurt by the decline of the Chesapeake Bay crab population.

Wittman has sought federal aid to install cameras in Prince William County police cars to protect the county against allegations of racial profiling in its enforcement of laws directed at illegal immigrants.

He co-sponsored legislation to give federal recognition to six Virginia Indian tribes, a measure the House Natural Resources Committee endorsed in April 2009.

Wittman made a trip to Iraq in January 2008. He said the Iraqi government was beginning to create "bits and pieces of a democracy" and that the country is much more secure. He also praised Iraqi Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki, whom many other lawmakers had criticized for not moving more aggressively on political reforms.

Since his first election, Wittman has easily turned aside challengers in the conservative district that takes in some of the most treasured sites from the nation's founding.

In 2012, he faces a challenge from little-known Democrat Adam Cook and from Glenda Gail Parker, a perennial candidate running as an Independent Green whose campaigns have always been focused on the issue of expanded passenger train service.
