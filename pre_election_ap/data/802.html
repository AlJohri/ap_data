Sandy Levin was born in Detroit and now resides in Royal Oak, Mich. He earned a bachelor's degree from the University of Chicago in 1952, a master's degree in international relations from Columbia University in 1954 and a law degree from Harvard University in 1957.

Levin served on the Oakland County Board of Supervisors from 1961 to 1964 and in the state Senate from 1965 to 1970. He was chairman of the Michigan Democratic Party in 1968 and 1969.

Levin was a fellow at Harvard's Kennedy School of Government in 1975 and assistant administrator of the Agency for International Development from 1977 to 1981.

He was elected to the U.S. House in 1982.

Levin's wife, Victoria, died in 2008 after battling breast cancer. The couple has four children. In 2012, he married Pamela Cole, a professor of clinical child psychology and human development at Pennsylvania State University.

Profile

Sandy Levin, even after 30 years in Congress, retains a taste for confrontation and a skill for rhetoric. Although he can forge compromises, the longtime representative more often finds himself admonishing critics and standing up for the auto industry, which is important to his Michigan district.

Fellow Democratic Rep. Marcy Kaptur of Ohio has described him as "a pit bull with manners."

Levin took over as chairman of the Ways and Means Committee in 2010 and in the process picked up a higher profile after decades in the shadow of his younger brother, U.S. Sen. Carl Levin. He became the committee's ranking member after that year's election, in which the Republicans took control of the chamber.

Responding to the controversy over whether 2012 Republican presidential candidate Mitt Romney should release his tax returns, Sandy Levin introduced legislation that would require any candidate for the White House to release a decade's worth of tax returns.

Levin has been a strong ally of labor unions at the heart of trade, Social Security and welfare battles and a point person for the auto industry during federal bailouts and precipitous drops in auto sales. Throughout his career, the congressman has remained close to his brother, who is chairman of the Senate Armed Services Committee and was elected to the Senate in 1978.

The brothers were major players in 2009 in keeping General Motors and Chrysler afloat through government intervention, and they worked to fund the so-called Cash for Clunkers rebate program meant to stimulate auto sales during the economic downturn. They bear such a resemblance that Carl Levin keeps a "confusion file" with funny snippets of their mistaken identity.

Levin has pushed for improved labor rights and environmental protections in trade agreements and has kept a close eye on relations with China and South Korea, countries closely tied to the automotive industry and its markets.

"I'm from Michigan. I've seen firsthand the dislocation from globalization," Levin said in 2007 as the House approved a trade deal with Peru with tougher labor and environmental standards.

In 2012, during an election-year impasse over whether to extend the full range of the Bush-era tax cuts or allow them to expire for the wealthiest of Americans, Levin argued for the latter.

"The only way to finance these massive tax cuts for the highest earners is to eliminate or significantly curtail provisions that support the middle class," he said.

Levin helped marshal opposition to the Social Security reforms sought by President George W. Bush and Republicans in 2005. He said privatizing the New Deal-era retirement program would destroy it.

Levin, a decade earlier, won changes to Republican-led welfare reforms that included health insurance guarantees and child support for welfare recipients who went to work.
