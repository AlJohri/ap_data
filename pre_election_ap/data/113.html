Grace Napolitano was raised in Brownsville, Texas, and moved to California after high school. She now resides in Norwalk.

She worked as a secretary for the California Department of Employment and the Ford Motor Co. She then became active in local politics and was elected to the Norwalk City Council in 1986, becoming mayor in 1989.

She was elected to the state Assembly in 1992 and was elected to the U.S. House in 1998.

Napolitano and her husband, Frank, have five children.

Profile

Grace Napolitano broke a campaign promise to voters in California's 38th Congressional District and yet they sent her back to Congress without opposition.

Napolitano signed a pledge in 1998 declaring she would not serve in the House for more than three terms. But the Democrat changed her mind in 2004. Voters, it seemed, didn't mind and returned Napolitano to Congress, where her agenda closely matches her constituents' concerns about water and transportation.

Due to redistricting following the 2010 census, Napolitano is running in 2012 in the 32nd Congressional District. She will face Republican David Miller in the November 2012 general election.

Napolitano's district in east Los Angeles faces contamination from military fuel that threatens groundwater supplies. She has pushed through more than $70 million in funding to clean up the San Gabriel River Basin.

Napolitano promotes water recycling, desalination and groundwater management as solutions to California's water needs.

She chairs the House Natural Resources subcommittee on water and power, advocating for investments in state and federal programs that improve the nation's water infrastructure.

The House in June 2010 passed a bill she sponsored that would allow the Hoover Dam to continue providing power to current customers through 2067 and would grant new access for Native American tribes that were previously excluded. Allocations of power to utilities in the Southwest were set to expire in 2017, and the reauthorization would allow agencies to prepare for changes and ensure uninterrupted delivery of electricity to customers.

Napolitano has advanced projects that address the congestion and pollution in her district, which is the primary commuting corridor for the nation's busiest port complex in Los Angeles and Long Beach.

Napolitano urged U.S. Postmaster General Patrick Donahoe to reconsider a consolidation that would have closed mail processing centers in her district.

She secured funding to expand Interstate 5, a major trucking route, and helped draft the Federal Railroad Safety Improvement Act. The law, passed in 2008, mandates the installation of a "positive train control" system to stop trains on a collision course. It was prompted by a deadly collision between a Southern California commuter train and freight train in September 2008.

Napolitano has pressed for mental health programs for veterans, minorities and children in her role as co-chair of the Congressional Mental Health Caucus. The House passed in May 2010 a resolution introduced by Napolitano designating May as mental health month. She also authored a bill that would provide mental health services in schools.

In 2012, Napolitano sent a letter to Health and Human Services Secretary Kathleen Sebelius and other top federal officials urging the Obama Administration to fully implement the Paul Wellstone and Pete Domenici Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act. The legislation, signed into law in 2008, ensures mental health and addiction treatment are covered the same as treatment for other medical illnesses and conditions.

Napolitano is a former chair of the Congressional Hispanic Caucus and a member of the U.S.-Mexico Congressional Caucus. She is a vocal proponent of immigration reform.
